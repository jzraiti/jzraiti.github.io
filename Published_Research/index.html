Current Research

Spore Shape and Ecological Niche in Amanita

One of the central challenges in studying fungi is figuring out what ecological roles different species play. With millions of species still undescribed, it’s often impossible to rely on traditional natural history alone. That’s why I’m especially interested in how physical traits—like the size and shape of spores—can give us clues about how fungi live, move, and interact with the world.

My current research focuses on the genus Amanita, a group that includes both decomposers and ectomycorrhizal species and spans an incredible diversity of spore shapes. Some spores are almost perfectly round; others are long and narrow. I’m investigating whether those shapes are just evolutionary accidents—or if they actually tell us something meaningful about how different species disperse or what niches they occupy.

To answer this, I’m combining morphometric analysis with phylogenetic modeling across a dataset of over 200 Amanita species. I’m testing whether spore shape patterns align with ecological strategies like symbiosis or saprotrophy, and whether closely related species tend to share similar spore traits. The hope is to uncover broader principles that link morphology to ecological function in fungi—principles that could help us understand fungal diversity at scale.

This project is ongoing, and I'm excited to share more as it develops. It's part of a broader effort to make sense of fungal form and function in an era of big data and rapid discovery.
